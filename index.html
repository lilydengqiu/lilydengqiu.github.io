Introduction¶

Welcome to this data science pipeline tutorial! We'll be going over the basics of setting up, getting data, tidying the data, managing missing values, data analysis and visualization, and hypothesis testing.

In this tutorial, we'll be using data on unemployment rates and median salaries for different college majors. The dataset is also organized into three categories: graduate students, non-graduate students, and total students.

Picking a college major is hard, there's so many things to consider! For some people, it's important to consider how financially stable and how easily one could potentially find a job with a degree in a certain major. In addition to this, some people don't know whether or not they should attend graduate school. Will they more easily find a job and earn more money if they become a graduate student?

Within this tutorial, we hope to answer these two questions:

  1. How does college major influence employment and how much money one makes?
  2. What's the relationship between graduate student salaries and non-graduate student salaries?

Setting Up¶

First, we will need to import some packages for this tutorial. These packages will help us more easily move, modify, and change data in the forms of arrays, tables, graphs, and more! Specifically, we'll be using pandas to organize our data in a dataframe and we'll use matplotlib to plot some graphs.

Missing Data¶

Checking for missing data is very important! We don't want our later results to give us problems because of missing values. To deal with these missing values, we can either replace or delete them. In our case, because there is no clear relationship between different rows or column categories, it is not easy to assume and replace missing values. Instead, we will delete any possible missing values.

First, we will see if any value in our dataframe is NaN. NaN is how the Pandas library signifies missing or null values.

In [121]:

From the code above, we see that there are no rows with any NaNs. Because we have no NaNs, we don't need to modify our dataframe for NaNs. However, there can be other forms of missing data. In this case, missing data in our dataframe can be represented by values of 0.0 for all of our type-float columns. If employment rates, salary medians, or number of students have the value of 0.0, that would mess up our analysis. The 0.0s in our data could interfere with calculations in our analysis, causing inaccuracy. Therefore, we consider 0.0s missing data, and we will remove rows containing them.

Hypothesis Testing¶

What is hypothesis testing? Well, in this case, it's statistical method that will helps us determine whether or not there is a linear relationship between Nongraduate Median Salary and Graduate Median Salary. But generically, hypothesis testing is a statistical inference method that will help us draw conclusions about a population given data randomly sampled from that population at a specific significance level. For more information on hypothesis testing (including information on signficant value, null hypotheses, and alternate hypothesis), you can click on this link: https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.105.586461

For this tutorial, we'll be using a significance level of 0.05, which is denoted 𝛼=0.05.

The null hypothesis: 𝐻𝑜 = There is no linear relationship between Nongraduate Median Salary and Graduate Median Salary.

The alternate hypothesis: 𝐻𝑎 = There is a linear relationship between Nongraduate Median Salary and Graduate Median Salary.

Given the information from the summary table, we see that the p-value of the Nongraduate Median Salary coefficient is 0.0. At the 𝛼=0.05 significance level, the p-value 0.0 is less than 𝛼. Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis of no linear relationship between Graduate Median Salary and Nongraduate Median Salary. So, there is a linear relationship between Graduate Median Salary and Nongraduate Median Salary. In this case, we cannot conclude (nor would it be reasonable to assume) that an increase in Nongraduate Median Salary causes an increase in Graduate Median Salary.

However, we can conclude that an increase in Nongraduate Median Salary can correlate to an increase in Graduate Median Salary assuming all else is constant. This correlation could be reasoned that for many different majors, the Graduate Median Salary is higher than the Non-graduate Median Salary (which we found in the Data Analysis and Visualization section of this tutorial); from the linear regression equation, Graduate Median Salary is higher by a fixed value of 23,318 dollars and increases by 0.91 dollars for every dollar increase in Non-graduate Median Salary. This positive correlation could signify that we could possibly predict the Graduate Median Salary for a major given the Non-graduate Median Salary. This idea could be answered through machine learning— which would be for another tutorial.
